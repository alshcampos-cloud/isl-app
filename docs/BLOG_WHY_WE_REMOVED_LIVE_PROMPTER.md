# Why we removed our Live Prompter

*By Alsh Campos, founder, InterviewAnswers.ai*
*April 23, 2026*

---

Six months ago I shipped a feature called the Live Prompter. It was designed
to help you during an interview. You would open it on your phone or second
monitor, it would listen to the interviewer's question, and it would surface
bullet points from your prepared answers in real time. A soft safety net for
the moments when your mind went blank.

People asked for it. I built it. It worked.

Last week I took the live mode out.

This is the longer version of why.

---

## I kept meeting hiring managers who were angry

I didn't plan to write a manifesto about ethics. I was just trying to build
something useful. But over the last three months I had enough conversations
with enough hiring managers and career coaches that I couldn't ignore the
pattern.

The pattern was: they had been burned. A candidate crushed the Zoom
interview. Every answer was polished, structured, on-theme. They made the
offer. And then, in the first week on the job, the person couldn't do any
of the things they had talked so confidently about in the interview.

The hiring manager felt stupid. The team felt betrayed. And quietly, in
HR circles, the story was starting to surface: it wasn't that the
candidate was a bad hire. It was that the candidate had been using an AI
copilot during the interview.

There's a category of tool now &mdash; Final Round AI, Cluely,
Interview.chat, and others &mdash; explicitly marketing themselves as
"undetectable" real-time interview assistants. They don't dance around it.
The pitch is: we help you cheat. More politely: we help you deliver
answers you couldn't deliver on your own.

That category is growing fast. And the hiring-manager backlash is growing
right alongside it.

When I looked at my Live Prompter honestly, I had to admit: it was
functionally the same thing. Dressed differently, sold as "help during
interviews," marketed to anxious candidates who just wanted to not
freeze. But functionally &mdash; same category. Same ethical problem.
Same risk to the user.

---

## The companies I admire all refused something

Once I started looking at this as a strategic question rather than a
feature question, I started seeing a pattern.

**DuckDuckGo** refused to track users. They did it publicly, on a
billboard on Highway 101 in 2011, while Google was printing money off ad
targeting. It took a decade for that stance to pay off &mdash; but when
the Snowden leaks hit in 2013, DDG was the only search engine prepared to
catch the defectors.

**Signal** refused to monetize through ads or data. They structured the
company as a non-profit foundation so they literally couldn't flip. When
WhatsApp changed its privacy policy in January 2021, Signal gained 40
million users in a single week. The refusal held for a decade, then
compounded violently when the cultural moment arrived.

**Apple** refused to let apps track users without consent. They called
out "the data industrial complex" by name, on billboards, in op-eds.
Meta lost an estimated $10 billion in 2022 ad revenue. Apple turned that
criticism into more marketing.

**Basecamp** refused to chase growth. They refused integrations. They
refused open opens in HEY email. Every refusal was a blog post, a public
commitment, a structural constraint. Not a tagline.

**Patagonia** ran a full-page ad in the New York Times on Black Friday
2011 telling people not to buy their flagship jacket. Revenue grew 30%
the following year.

The pattern is: the most durable tech brands are defined as much by what
they refuse to do as by what they build. The refusal is structural, not
marketing. And when the cultural moment arrives &mdash; and it always
arrives &mdash; they are the only prepared beneficiary.

---

## What we refuse to build

So here's our version, on the record.

We do not and will not build real-time interview assistance tools.

We do not and will not market "undetectable" features.

We do not and will not build anything designed to be used during a live
interview, a pre-recorded video interview, or any evaluative process
where the employer has not authorized AI assistance.

That commitment is in our Terms of Service now, not just our marketing.
It's in our product roadmap as a non-goal. It's on our ethics page at
[interviewanswers.ai/ethics](https://www.interviewanswers.ai/ethics). It
will be on a billboard the day we can afford one.

---

## What we're building instead

The Live Prompter is dead. In its place we have the Practice Prompter
&mdash; a rehearsal tool for the hours before the interview, where you
answer questions out loud, get feedback, and build the muscle memory that
actually carries you through the interview when it counts.

That's the whole bet.

The single best predictor of how you'll do in an interview is not the
sophistication of the tools you bring into the room. It's how many times
you've answered the question out loud, alone, before. That's not
glamorous. It's not magic. It's what actually works.

If you get the offer and a hiring manager asks, months later, whether you

used InterviewAnswers.ai to practice. And that's it."

No asterisks. No "well, technically&hellip;"

That's the product we're building.
