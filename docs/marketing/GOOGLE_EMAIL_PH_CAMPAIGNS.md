## LinkedIn Post 2: "Why I Built an AI Interview Coach"

```
I want to tell you about the worst interview I ever had.

I was applying for a role I was overqualified for. I knew the industry. I had the experience. I'd even done the exact work they were hiring for.

The interviewer asked: "Tell me about a time you had to influence a stakeholder without direct authority."

I had three perfect examples. I froze on all of them. What came out was a rambling two-minute non-answer where I said "um" fourteen times and forgot to mention what actually happened.

I didn't get the job.

That night I thought: I don't have a knowledge problem. I have a practice problem. I've never said these answers out loud to another person before.

Think about it. Athletes practice before games. Musicians rehearse before concerts. Lawyers do mock trials. But job seekers? We read sample answers on the internet and hope for the best.

That's why I built InterviewAnswers.ai.

It's an AI that acts as your interviewer. It asks you questions. You answer out loud. It tells you what worked and what didn't. It scores your answers on the STAR framework that interviewers actually evaluate you on.

The AI doesn't know your industry better than you do. It's not trying to. It's doing what a good coach does — making you practice the delivery until the right answer comes out clearly under pressure.

Three things I learned building this:

1. Saying an answer out loud is 10x harder than thinking about it. Every user tells us this on their first session.

2. Structure matters more than content. Interviewers remember organized answers even if the story isn't dramatic.

3. Confidence is a skill, not a trait. It comes from repetition. Three practice sessions and people sound different.

If you're interviewing soon: interviewanswers.ai. Free to try.

If you're not: I bet you know someone who is. Share this with them.
```

## LinkedIn Post 3: "The Nursing Interview Gap Nobody Talks About"

```
Nurses are some of the most rigorously trained professionals in any industry.

Years of clinical rotations. Board exams. Continuing education. Direct patient care experience that most white-collar workers can't even imagine.

And then they walk into a job interview and get asked: "Tell me about a time you had a conflict with a coworker."

Here's the gap nobody talks about:

Nursing education teaches you clinical skills. It does not teach you how to interview. And nursing interviews are different from every other industry.

They ask you to describe clinical scenarios using communication frameworks like SBAR (Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation). They want to hear about patient advocacy, interdisciplinary collaboration, and de-escalation in life-or-death settings.

But there's no standardized way to practice this. New grad nurses are applying for their first RN role with zero interview prep tools built for them. Experienced nurses switching from ICU to OR are Googling "nursing interview questions" and getting the same ten generic questions recycled across every blog.

My co-founder is a clinical nurse working in infection prevention. She told me this gap exists. At first I thought it was a niche thing.

Then we looked at the numbers. "Nursing interview questions" gets searched tens of thousands of times per month. And the resources available are blog posts with sample answers that weren't written by nurses.

So we built a nursing interview track inside InterviewAnswers.ai.

What makes it different:

-- 70+ questions written and reviewed by healthcare professionals
-- SBAR communication drills (timed, with AI feedback)
-- Clinical scenario practice where the AI coaches your delivery, not your clinical knowledge
-- Grounded in published frameworks: NCSBN Clinical Judgment Model, Nursing Process, Maslow's hierarchy

One thing we are deliberately careful about: the AI does not generate clinical content. Every question and scenario comes from our curated, human-reviewed library. The AI coaches how you communicate — structure, specificity, confidence, clarity. It does not evaluate clinical accuracy. That's for your education and your experience to cover.

If you're a nurse prepping for an interview: interviewanswers.ai/nursing

If you work in healthcare recruiting or nursing education and have thoughts on this gap — I'd genuinely love to hear from you in the comments. We're building this with the community, not in isolation.
```
